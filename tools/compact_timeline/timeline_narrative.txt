- [1194-10-02] Whispers of a Black Coach - Word reached Sherwood of a black coach seen crossing the Nottingham road only at night, its horses never tiring, never needing rest. Three villagers from Edwinstowe had vanished within a week, and the survivors spoke only of red eyes at their windows and a cold that settled into the bones before sleep took them. Robin gathered the band at the great oak to hear the accounts firsthand. *Little John* was skeptical, calling it "sheriff's mischief dressed up as ghost tales" to keep the forest folk indoors after dark. But Friar Tuck, pale as he recounted it, insisted this was no invention of Prince John's men—he had seen the same signs once before, years ago, on pilgrimage in the eastern mountains.

- [1194-10-03] Tuck's Warning - By firelight, Friar Tuck told what he knew. In the Carpathians he had heard monks speak in hushed tones of a *voivode* who had made a pact to cheat death itself—a nobleman turned into something that fed on the living and could not be killed by sword or arrow alone. The monks called him **Dracul's son**, and said he traveled west when his homeland grew too small for his hunger. "If he has come to England," Tuck said, "then Prince John's tyranny is the least of Nottingham's troubles." Robin, ever practical, asked only one question: *what kills it?* Tuck did not have a full answer—only fragments. Sunlight. Running water. Wood driven through the heart. And silver, blessed silver, which burned such creatures like fire burns straw.

- [1194-10-06] The Silver Arrows - Robin sent Much the Miller's Son to every church poor-box and silversmith between Sherwood and Nottingham, trading arrows and coin for anything silver that could be melted down. Will Scarlet grumbled that they were "arming against a fairy tale," but helped all the same, hammering the metal into arrowheads late into the night. By dawn, Robin had a dozen silver-tipped arrows—not enough, he feared, but a start.

- [1194-10-09] Ambush at Blidworth Crossing - The black coach came at last, exactly as the villagers described, its wheels silent on the frozen road. Robin's band lay in wait at Blidworth Crossing, arrows nocked. What stepped from the coach was no ordinary lord. Tall, gaunt, dressed in old finery gone to rot at the edges, the man who called himself only "the Count" moved between one blink and the next, closing thirty paces in a heartbeat. He struck down two of Robin's men before a single arrow found him—and even then, only a silver shaft to the shoulder made him recoil, hissing, smoke curling from the wound. The Count vanished into the trees rather than press the fight, but not before fixing his gaze on Robin and speaking a single promise: *"I will remember this face."*

- [1194-10-14] Marian's Counsel - Maid Marian, hearing of the ambush, rode to Sherwood against Robin's wishes to see him unharmed. She brought news from the castle as well: the Sheriff of Nottingham, unaware of the true nature of his new "guest," had granted the Count lodging at an abandoned keep at Papplewick, believing him a foreign nobleman useful for taxing the shire further. Marian argued that the band could not fight a creature of the night with arrows alone forever. She proposed drawing the Count into open ground at midday, when his power was said to be weakest, rather than waiting for him to choose the hour.

- [1194-10-19] The Siege of Papplewick Keep - At noon, Robin's band struck the keep at Papplewick. Little John broke the gate with a felled oak trunk while Tuck, censer in hand, chanted every rite he could recall to weaken the creature's hold on the shadows within. Marian led a second party to fire the west wing, flooding the halls with daylight through broken shutters. The Count met them in the great hall, weakened but far from harmless—he threw Little John across the room with contemptuous ease and nearly reached Robin's throat before Tuck drove a burning brand into his side. It bought Robin the half-second he needed to loose three silver arrows in succession into the Count's chest. The creature did not die easily. It laughed, even as it burned, and swore that "the forest would remember him" before crumbling to ash and shadow, scattering on the wind that poured through the broken windows.

- [1194-10-20] The Ashes of Papplewick - The morning after, Robin's band returned to the keep to find only ash where the Count had fallen, and the black coach abandoned in the courtyard, its horses gone. Villagers from Edwinstowe came at dusk to see the ruin for themselves, many weeping with relief, a few leaving small offerings of garlic and wildflowers at the gate out of old habit rather than necessity. Robin kept one silver arrowhead, unfired, as a reminder—not of the victory, but of how close the band had come to losing far more than a fight against the Sheriff's soldiers.
